Family Meals

Vietnamese families eat home-cook food most of the time, especially lunch and dinner meals. There are many components that make up a meal, and are shared by everyone.

A typical Vietnamese home-cook meal consists of:

  • Freshly cooked rice in a rice cooker
  • One or two savory dishes
  • One vegetable dish
  • A large bowl of soup
  • A small dipping sauce bowl

Tet (New Year) Feast

Northern Vietnam

Northern Vietnamese Tet feast includes: bánh chưng (sticky rice), thịt đông (jelly meat), nem (egg rolls), nộm (pickled vegetables), xôi (sticky rice), giò lụa (meat loaf), thịt gà luộc (a whole boiled chicken)

Central Vietnam

Central Vietnamese Tet feast includes: bánh tét (cylinder sticky rice), ram cuốn (mini egg rolls), dưa món (pickle vegetables), mắm tôm chua (sour shrimp paste), thịt ngâm nước mắm (fish sauce dipped meat)

Southern Vietnam

Southern Vietnamese Tet feast also includes: bánh tét (cylinder sticky rice), thịt kho hột vịt (braised pork and duck eggs), canh khổ qua (bitter melon soup), củ kiệu (pickle onions), gỏi (mixed pickled vegetable), chả giò (egg rolls)

Snacks

Bánh Tráng (Rice Paper)

Rice paper salad is a snack that originated from Tay Ninh, Vietnam, it's a very popular snack among Vietnamese students. Mixed rice paper salad consists of rice paper strips, fried dried shrimps, beef jerky, herbs, young papaya strips, peanuts and savory sauce.

Chè

Chè is a general Vietnamese word to describe all Vietnamese sweet beverage, which is usually in the form of pudding, or plain drink. It corporates fruit, beans, seeds, tapioca, glutinous rice and coconut milk.

Cá Viên (Fish Balls)

Originated from China, fried fish balls have made it names in Vietnam and became extremely popular among children. Vietnamese usually enjoy the fish balls with peanuts hoisin sauce.

Phá Lấu (Intestine Stew)

Phá Lấu is a Vietnamese dish originated from southern China. It's made of pork, and offal such as intestine, braised in spiced stock. Small wooden sticks are used to pick up the meat.

Ốc (Snails)

Snail is one of Vietnamese all-time favorite dishes, it's a low-carb food and dense in protein. Snails are usually cooked in different sauces such as rich buttery sauce, sweet and sour tamarine sauce. Despite its chewy texture, many people enjoy it.

Nước Mía (Sugar Cane Drink)

Sugar cane drink is made from crushing sugarcane stalks in a machine, either hand-cranked or electric, with no added sugar. Sme vendors show their creativity by adding kumquat, orange, pineapple, and lime.

Hột Vịt Lộn (Balut/Duck Fetus)

Hột Vịt Lộn is another low-carb street snack that familiar with Vietnamese and Filipino culture. It's basically fertilized duck eggs, some version would be quail eggs. The dish is eaten with salt and pepper, and a saide of rau răm, a type of Vietnamese herb.

Kẹo Kéo (Hand-pull Candy)

Kẹo Kéo is a cheap candy, often sold on the street to little children and sweet-tooth students. It is made by hand with molasses, sugar and peanuts. Some candy sellers are also street performers, they would sing or play music while making the candies.

Veganism

Then

Influenced from Buddhism for more than 100 years, Vietnamese culture has considered veganism as something exclusively to Buddhist monks. Today, Buddist followers still practice veganism on the 15th day of the Lunar calendar.

Now

Nowsaday, more and more young people have shown interest in going vegan for health benefits and animal rights. More vegan restaurants without the influece of Buddhism are open to public.
